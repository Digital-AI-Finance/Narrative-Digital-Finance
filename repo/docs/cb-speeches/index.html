Research Overview

This research investigates whether Federal Reserve speech sentiment (hawkish vs. dovish) correlates with underlying macroeconomic conditions. Using 2,421 US Fed speeches from 1996-2025 and six FRED macroeconomic indicators, we construct PCA-based indices and analyze their relationship with speech sentiment.

Key Finding

Near-zero correlation (0.005) between CB speech sentiment and macro indices - a significant empirical result challenging assumptions about central bank communication effectiveness and the narrative-driven policy transmission mechanism.

Methodology

Data Sources

  • FRED Macroeconomic Data: Fed Funds Rate, CPI, PPI, GDP, Unemployment, Nonfarm Payrolls (1996-2025)
  • CB Speeches: BIS/Gigando dataset with NER annotations and sentiment labels

Analysis Pipeline

  • PCA: Dimensionality reduction on 6 macro indicators to construct Macro Strength Index (PC1) and Inflation Index (PC2)
  • PELT Algorithm: Structural break detection with RBF kernel (penalty=4)
  • Rolling Regression: 36-month window beta and R-squared calculation
  • Sentiment Aggregation: Monthly hawkish/dovish counts with 12-month rolling standardization

PCA Component Interpretation

  • PC1 (Macro Strength): Fed Rate, Nonfarm Payrolls (+), Unemployment (-) - captures expansion/tightening cycles
  • PC2 (Inflation): CPI, PPI (+), Fed Rate (-) - captures price pressure independent of policy
  • PC3 (Policy-Labor Conflict): Fed Rate, Unemployment (+), Payrolls (-) - policy tightening amid labor weakness

Implications

  • Policy Independence: CB communication may be more forward-looking than reactive to current conditions
  • Narrative Disconnect: Speech sentiment does not mirror macroeconomic reality in real-time
  • Mean Reversion: Strong negative autocorrelation (-0.43) in hawkish sentiment suggests rapid narrative shifts
  • Research Gap: Challenges assumptions about the effectiveness of narrative-driven policy transmission
