1.1 Achievement of Research Objectives

5,892 / 6,000 characters

The project addressed three central research questions: (1) In what sense are financial markets ex-ante predictable? (2) Is ex-ante forecastability persistent and applicable for real use cases? (3) How can structural break detection improve modern portfolio theory? We achieved substantial progress across four work packages, with methodological innovations and six publications produced.

WP1: Text Data and Text Analytics

We developed comprehensive NLP frameworks for financial text analysis addressing how textual analysis can be efficiently used for portfolio management. The TOPol (Topic-Orientation Polarity) framework provides a semi-unsupervised approach for reconstructing multidimensional semantic polarity fields using transformer embeddings, UMAP projection, and Leiden clustering. Unlike traditional unidimensional sentiment scoring, TOPol captures the full complexity of financial narratives through vectorial representation of polarity shifts. The framework embeds documents using transformer-based LLMs, applies neighbor-tuned UMAP projection, and segments via Leiden partitioning. Given regime boundaries, it computes directional vectors between topic-boundary centroids, producing polarity fields that capture semantic displacement. Code and data are publicly available (OSF: osf.io/nr94j). Additionally, we created an AI-enhanced systematic literature review methodology using NLP techniques, clustering algorithms, and interpretability tools, currently under revision at Financial Innovation. Custom data pipelines were developed for RavenPack financial news, LSEG earnings transcripts, BIS central bank speeches, and SEC EDGAR 10-K/10-Q filings.

WP2: Structural Breaks Detection and Asset Price Bubbles

Following the proposed three-step approach, we developed methodologies for detecting structural breaks in financial markets. The Macro Narratives Framework combines a PCA-based US Macro Strength Index (using FED Funds Rate, CPI, PPI, GDP, Unemployment, Nonfarm Payrolls from St. Louis FED FRED, 1996-2025) with the PELT algorithm for change point detection. The first principal component captures joint dynamics in growth, inflation, and labor conditions. As validation, we applied the framework to Federal Reserve banker speech transcripts (BIS Gigando datasets, 2004-2010) around the 2008 financial crisis, successfully identifying narrative shifts at detected breakpoints using BERTopic topic modeling. Null hypothesis testing via 1,000 random shuffling simulations validated statistical significance of observed polarity drift (FinBERT sentiment), semantic centroid movement (cosine similarity), and keyword evolution (Jaccard similarity of MMR sets). The framework primarily demonstrates post-ante detection capability; ex-ante live prediction remains an ongoing validation target for future research.

WP3: Narratives for Structural Breaks

The original proposal included a 2x2 experimental design to test narrative effects on investment decisions. This methodology evolved toward computational approaches providing broader scalability. The Multimodal Influence paper (SSRN 4698153) extends traditional NLP to include non-textual data (images, video, audio) for financial instrument pricing using Generative AI, addressing exponential data growth from dozens of exabytes in 2003 to approximately 180 zettabytes by 2025. The TOPol framework moves beyond unidimensional sentiment to capture multidimensional semantic polarity through transformer embeddings and Leiden clustering. BERTopic-based narrative shift analysis quantifies polarity drift, semantic centroid movement, and keyword evolution across economic regimes. Word clouds aggregate MMR keywords from all topics before and after breakpoints, visualizing thematic drift across narrative regimes. This computational pivot enabled analysis at scale that experimental designs could not achieve.

WP4: Multidimensional AI and ML Solutions

In collaboration with Deutsche Borse AG (Dr. Stefan Schlamp, Head of Quantitative Analytics), we developed novel methodology for classifying market participants based on nanosecond-level reaction times, extending WP4's objective of integrating multiple AI/ML approaches. The HFT Market Microstructure framework uses nanosecond-level timestamp data from Deutsche Borse's T7 platform to distinguish Ultra-Fast Traders (UFT, less than 1 microsecond using FPGAs), High-Frequency Traders (HFT, 1-10 microseconds), and conventional participants (greater than 10 microseconds). Research documents participation shares (UFT/HFT produce roughly 20% of total participation), price discovery via 15-second mark-out signal-to-noise ratio, and market quality metrics including order-imbalance volatility, Amihud illiquidity, and high-frequency return diagnostics for Euro STOXX 50 Index Futures from January to August 2025. This industry collaboration enabled access to proprietary data unavailable through academic channels.

1.2 Challenges, Negative Results and Unexpected Outcomes

1,987 / 2,000 characters

Data Access Evolution

The original proposal envisioned using Bloomberg and Refinitiv terminals as primary data sources. We developed custom data pipelines for alternative sources: RavenPack (financial news), LSEG (earnings transcripts), BIS Gigando (central bank speeches), SEC EDGAR (regulatory filings), and Deutsche Borse (nanosecond trading data). These alternatives proved more suitable for narrative analysis research, providing both textual content and market reaction data unavailable through terminal-based access.

Methodological Evolution

The original WP3.1 design planned controlled 2x2 investment experiments to test how narratives sway investment opinions. This approach evolved toward the Multimodal Influence Framework (SSRN 4698153), providing computational analysis at scale. The pivot was driven by: (1) scalability requirements for analyzing exponentially growing data volumes, (2) reproducibility advantages of computational methods over experimental designs, and (3) opportunity to extend beyond text to multimodal analysis. However, the original question of whether narratives causally influence individual investment decisions remains unaddressed through behavioral experimentation.

Ex-ante vs Post-ante Gap

The original proposal emphasized live (ex-ante) detection of structural breaks. Our demonstrated achievements primarily validate post-ante detection capability using historical data (2008 crisis case study). Real-time prediction performance and out-of-sample validation remain gaps requiring future research.

Unexpected Opportunity

The Deutsche Borse collaboration (initiated January 2024) provided access to proprietary nanosecond-level trading data, enabling the HFT market microstructure research stream. This produced joint publications but expanded scope beyond the original narrative-focused objectives. The collaboration demonstrates industry relevance but represents partial mission evolution.

1.3 Contribution to Knowledge Advancement

1,978 / 2,000 characters

This research bridges narrative economics (Shiller, 2017, 2019) with computational linguistics and financial econometrics, providing quantitative tools for analyzing how narratives influence market dynamics. The work addresses a fundamental question: how do narrative forms influence volatility and regime changes in financial markets?

TOPol Framework

First semi-unsupervised framework for multidimensional semantic polarity in financial texts. Moves beyond unidimensional sentiment scoring to capture narrative complexity through transformer embeddings, UMAP projection, and Leiden clustering. The vectorial representation enables quantification of magnitude, direction, and semantic meaning of polarity shifts across discourse regimes. Open source release (osf.io/nr94j) enables replication and extension.

Macro-Narrative Integration

Novel combination of PCA-based macroeconomic indices (US Macro Strength Index from FRED data) with BERTopic topic modeling for detecting narrative regime shifts. Null hypothesis testing via 1,000 random shuffling simulations provides statistical validation. Quantifies polarity drift, semantic centroid movement, and keyword evolution at structural breakpoints.

HFT Classification Methodology

New latency-based taxonomy distinguishing UFT (less than 1 microsecond, FPGA-based), HFT (1-10 microseconds), and conventional participants (greater than 10 microseconds). Validated on Deutsche Borse T7 platform data with documented participation shares and market quality metrics for regulatory monitoring applications.

Impact Areas

Narrative Economics, Central Bank Communication Analysis, Market Microstructure, Volatility Regime Detection, NLP in Finance, High-Frequency Trading Research, Asset Bubble Detection, Regulatory Technology.
